| 28-revision-v1      | Fiction and non-fiction works are not the same. Different reading goals require different approaches.

I have always loved &lt;fiction author&gt;

In recent years, I've grown to love pop-sci books. Essayists like Malcolm Gladwell and David Foster Wallace embed insight into stori
es. They use anecdotes and experiences to persuade/inform readers of new                                                           

horizons. They cater to their audience with very high-level explanations, because the content allows for less rigor than other subje
cts.                                                                                                                               

Textbooks are a whole different ballgame.

Most textbooks give low-level descriptions of dense information. They hit readers with the data firehose, and expect them to filter,
 process, memorize, and understand it all on their own.                                                                            

Reading is an active process. Authors expect their audience to process information. But depending on the content, varying degrees of
 work are required from the audience. // fiction &lt;&lt; pop-sci &lt;&lt; textbooks &lt;&lt; reference                            

// the conceptual firehose (throughput)

For presenting difficult material, authors have two options: teach more thoroughly, or expect the reader to teach themselves. By tea
ching more thoroughly, authors reduce the pressure of the conceptual fir                                                           

ehose. They go slower, use lengthier explanations, give more examples, etc.

Recursive reading is the perfect approach for media that (1) has high conceptual throughput and (2) does not have spoilers.

// problem: conceptual throughput. stories are sequential. learning is top-down, bottom-up, and sequential.

***

Fiction and non-fiction works are not the same. Different reading goals require different approaches.

There’s generally two reasons to read:

* to experience a story

* to learn something new

Most texts meet both of these goals. Malcolm Gladwell does an amazing job of embedding insight in stories. But works like Holographi
c Reduced Representation don’t cater to readers; most non-fiction authors expect their audience to parse the information in whatever
 way necessary.                                                                                                                    

See the difference? Some books expect the reader to read, reread, perform practice problems, and consult outside materials in hopes 
of understanding the content. What should we do in these cases?                                                                    

***

Traditional (iterative) reading is a linear process. Readers are expected to read each chapter, in order, at maximum comprehension.

The traditional approach makes sense for works of fiction. Authors stitch stories together to maximize coherency and suspense. // It
 is best to assume that authors know what they’re doing; follow their lead when reading for pleasure.                              

Textbooks offer similar requirements. Chapter 9 makes more sense after reading chapter 8.
